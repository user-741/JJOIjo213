Session 1: Principles and Foundation of Cybersecurity

Introduction to Information Security & User Responsibilities

  • Information Security Definition: Tasks of securing digital information manipulated by microprocessors, stored on devices, or transmitted over networks
  • CIA Triad: Confidentiality (approved access), Integrity (accuracy), Availability (accessibility)
  • End-User Role: You are the "last line of defense" with responsibilities for passwords, computer security, and data protection
  • Security Threats: Malware, phishing, spyware, spam
  • Practical Measures: Password management, email security, computer protection
  • Legal Framework: Introduction to Privacy Act, Spam Act, Cybercrime Act

Emerging IT Trends and Technologies

  • Software Distribution: Pull technology (user-initiated) vs. Push technology (server-initiated)
  • Application Service Providers (ASPs): Provide software access for a fee
  • Software as a Service (SaaS): On-demand software with data stored on provider's servers
  • Biometrics: High-accuracy security using physical characteristics
  • Wireless Technologies: Wi-Fi (radio waves) and Bluetooth (PAN creation)
  • Computing Models: Grid computing (combined processing), Utility computing (pay-per-use), Cloud computing (IaaS, PaaS, SaaS)
  • Cloud Types: Public, Private, Hybrid, Community - chosen based on security needs
  • Nanotechnology: Manipulating materials at molecular level

The Cybersecurity Legal & Framework Landscape

  • Cybersecurity Definition: Principles and practices to safeguard computing assets against threats
  • User Responsibilities: Password management, account security, data protection, risk assessment
  • Voluntary Frameworks: ISO, NIST, COBIT, ISM, VPDSS
  • Legal Environment: Combination of voluntary codes and enforcement mechanisms

Session 2: Law and Information Security

Foundations of Law, Ethics, and Policy

  • Cultural Mores: Fixed morals or customs forming the basis of ethics
  • Ethics: Rules for socially acceptable behavior (not enforced by courts)
  • Laws: Rules that mandate/prohibit behavior (enforced by authorities)
  • Policy: Organizational laws defining acceptable workplace behavior
  • Key Terms: Jurisdiction, Long-arm jurisdiction, Case law, Liability, Due care, Due diligence
  • Law Types: Civil, Criminal, Tort, Private, Public
  • Information Security Role: Reduces litigation likelihood, demonstrates due care
  • International Laws: European Council Cyber-Crime Convention, GDPR
  • Ethical Challenges: Cultural differences, need for education and training

Professional Bodies and Policy Management

  • ACM (Association of Computing Machinery): World's first educational computing society with code of ethics
  • CSI (Computer Security Institute): Provides training and advocates for ethical behavior
  • Policy Importance: Starting point for security efforts, directs issue resolution
  • Policy Management: Requires regular reviews, responsible personnel, revision processes

Implementing Security: Classification, Training, and Architecture

  • Information Classification: Categorizing data (public, internal, classified)
  • Clean Desk Policy: Securing classified information at day's end
  • SETA Program: Security Education, Training, and Awareness to reduce accidental breaches
  • Security Architecture: Defence in depth (layered security), Security perimeter
  • Security Components: Firewalls, DMZ (Demilitarized Zone), IDS (Intrusion Detection Systems)

Session 3: Cyber Law Policy

Cyber Law Fundamentals and Data Privacy Regulations

  • Cyber Law Definition: Legal issues related to networked information devices and technologies
  • Need for Cyber Law: Cyberspace is intangible, borderless, high-volume, open, anonymous, and economically efficient
  • Privacy Act 1988: Australian law protecting personal information with 13 APPs
  • Key APPs: Open management (APP 1), Anonymity (APP 2), Collection limitations (APP 3), Security (APP 11), Access rights (APP 12)
  • OAIC: Office of the Australian Information Commissioner enforces privacy laws
  • GDPR: EU regulation with extraterritorial reach, heavy fines, "Right to be Forgotten"
  • APP vs. GDPR: Similar requirements but GDPR has stronger individual rights

A Taxonomy of Cybercrimes

  • Personal Cybercrimes:
    • Harassment (cyberbullying between minors, cyber-harassment between adults)
    • Cyber-stalking (pattern of harassment with credible threat)
    • Phishing (fraudulent emails to steal credentials)
    • Pharming (redirecting to fake websites)
    • Identity theft (using personal information for gain)
  • Social Network Attacks:
    • Adware and malware
    • Clickjacking (malicious links posting unwanted content)
    • Malicious script scams
    • Fraud and shill bidding
    • Keyloggers (capturing typed information)
  • Cybercrimes Against Organizations:
    • Hacking: White-hat (ethical), Black-hat (malicious), Gray-hat (illegal but not malicious)
    • Hacktivism (political statement hacking)
    • Data breaches (unauthorized data access)
    • Cyber-terrorism
